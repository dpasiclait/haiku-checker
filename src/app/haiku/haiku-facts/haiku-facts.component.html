What is a Haiku?
Poem of "cutting words"

A haiku is a short poem made up of three phrases that adhere to a specific structure. Although there are different structures, the most well known is the 5 - 7 - 5 "cutting word" scheme.

Simply put, "cutting words" refer to the amount of syllables each line, called verse, must possess to form a haiku:

five (5) syllables for the first line

seven (7) syllables for the second line

five (5) syllables for the third line

Subject of haiku typically range from elements of nature to natural events. Most popular themes are the seasons and the change they bring, both in the physical plane as well as the spiritual.

From 17th Century Japan...
To the 21st Century Classroom
Initially used as the opening stanza, called Hokku , of other poem in early 17th century Japan, the haiku began to gain enough popularity in the mid 1600s to feature as a stand alone work of literature.

However, it wasn't until a century later that the Dutch traders introduced the Western world to this scheme of poetry, gaining enough popularity to inspire many English and Spanish haiku.

Soon, this form of literature found it's way into the modern school curriculum as English Lit teacher's favorite method of torture to inflict on poor unsuspecting students.
